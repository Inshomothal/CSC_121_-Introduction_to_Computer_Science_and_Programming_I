Section I
“Ordinary” People Are Building Peace with Conflict Tools

As introduced earlier, nonviolent resistance and social movements, like Black Lives Matter and Take Back the Streets, are much more effective in toppling dictatorship and ruthless oppression than violence—military, terrorist, or any other highly aggressive and organized violent effort. While individuals debate and doubt, a growing research body shows the truth. More and more case study examples are demonstrating the effectiveness of nonviolent strategic organizing in more and more countries around the world.

Restorative justice normally occurs after such destructive violence has occurred and many have suffered. So after nonviolent resistance ended apartheid in South Africa, truth and reconciliation occurred to rebuild the society. The reader can find myriad other examples.

On a smaller, more intimate scale, victim–offender–community mediation occurs after criminal violence when the victim(s) requests the opportunity to meet the offender(s), question them about their crime, educate them about how they harmed, and then negotiate repair of damage. Victims are hopefully made more whole; sometimes relationships are restored and the offender negotiates the conditions for reintegration into communities that are willing to consider and welcome.

While the following section focuses on introducing nonviolence and its power, the content is relevant to all aspects of restorative justice and peacebuilding—even daily living with gardens of delights.

Part 1

Introducing Second-Track Diplomacy

Citizen-led peacebuilding, nonviolent resistance, and their core tools.

Traditionally, when peace is mentioned, most think of what scholars refer to as negative peace—the absence of war. More recently, positive peace expands the idea to include citizen (second-track) and traditional peacebuilding. Peace rarely happens by surprise; it’s a purposeful choice built over time.

This section introduces second-track diplomacy alongside two widely used conflict-resolution processes: negotiation and mediation. We also hint at how facilitated, skillful mediation supports democracy building (good-governance research).

Definition — Second-Track (“People”) Diplomacy

Informal efforts to prevent war or restore society after war by developing cultures and institutions that sustain peace—distinct from first-track diplomacy (official state actors like heads of state and ambassadors).

High-profile NGOs and citizen activists are often visible in unofficial diplomacy, but many influential stakeholders work quietly in nonpublic forums. Students of multitrack diplomacy should ask: Who prevents conflict? Who is effectively mediating difference?

Icons like Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. and Archbishop Desmond Tutu illustrate one essential citizen process: nonviolent resistance—noncooperation with public exposure of injustice—executed with strong local and global support. Their examples show how ordinary people can join, create, and lead change.

Nonviolent resistance scales from micro-conflict interactions to community and political arenas. Parties can refuse to cooperate with behavior they oppose and, like Gandhi or Dr. King, consistently model higher ground—while building strategic alliances to amplify effective, nonviolent strategy.

Part 2

The Moral Ground of Noncooperation

Discipline, conscience, and the transformative power of refusing injustice.

The time is ripe to see a movement insisting on higher moral ground with global conflict resolution. If every person would discipline themselves to cooperate only with that—acts, relationships, employers, decisions—that reflect their values, principles, and ideals, one can imagine how incredibly transformative such noncooperation would be: for the individual, their groups, and society as a whole.

Yet even Gandhi, arguably the world’s greatest inspiration, strategist, and teacher of nonviolent resistance, would likely agree that India’s liberation from oppression was only the first step forward—and sadly, actually increased violence between warring Hindus and Muslims. Building peace requires more than independence, no matter how important liberation may be.

Reflection

The continuing violence in liberated Iraq after years of costly warfare brings this painful and troubling reality close to all Americans. Afghanistan is even worse. An honest witness to history is forced to admit this sobering truth: brute military force alone—or the threat of it—has not built peace that lasts or spreads through a society’s communities, poor as well as rich.

Government force—whether military or law enforcement—or violent freedom-fighting resistance, even domestic terrorism, can only play limited roles in building peace. Instead, what one finds in studying war and the military-industrial complex (see Paul Joseph’s Encyclopedia of War and Addiction to War) is that powerful politicians have manipulated and caused war for their own gain and profit. The rest of us—the majority of human beings—suffer and bear the costs.

The seeds of war and all violence are contained in our collective unconscious: the accumulated childhoods of generations and all cultures. While this book does not attempt to address these seeds beyond a brief introduction to what can help counter the effects of growing up in abusive, punitive, and neglectful homes without parental love and mentoring, neighborhoods terrorized by drug cartels and gangs, and schools where bullying and violence occur daily, it is critical that we acknowledge all we must heal and address as human beings to survive and eradicate violence and war.

Part 4

Flexibility and the Power of Informal Diplomacy

When civil society and independent initiatives bridge what formal systems cannot.

Civil society groups often act with greater flexibility than government bureaucracies, allowing them to respond swiftly and creatively to new issues and concerns. Because of their closeness to people, they can sense shifting realities and mobilize more quickly than traditional institutions. Visionary initiative frequently creates unexpected opportunities. Those who can operate with access to people and networks that can be mobilized rapidly can take the lead in times of change.

Example — The Oslo Accords

The Oslo Accords were preceded by months of informal communication preparing the ground for formal negotiation through relationship-building and identifying common ground. Increasingly, formal and informal diplomacy merge in this way.

The negotiation of South Africa’s transitional constitution brought official and track-two diplomacy together. It opened meetings to the public through a media campaign, made materials accessible online, and circulated a draft constitution for public review, comment, and objection. Such transparency embodied democratic participation.

Government and business both need independent watchdogs to oversee and stimulate their best performance. Nongovernmental organizations often serve this vital role. At its best, informal community diplomacy fills critical gaps in international capacity. Intranational and cross-ethnic disputes frequently fall outside international authority. Without track-two alternatives, minority groups may see violent struggle for independence as their only option.

With track-two diplomacy, participants can develop mutual understanding, transform destructive attitudes, build bridges between rights and responsibilities, and create lasting resolutions. Concrete ways for citizens to develop democratic capacities are increasingly common.

Emerging Global Trends

New international law and mechanisms now allow citizens to bring grievances—such as human-rights violations— outside their own state. Businesses craft arbitration processes to avoid national forums. The international community convenes truth-and-reconciliation commissions and other forums to investigate abuses by both citizens and leaders.

Restorative justice plays an important role in these emerging systems as a strong example of second-track diplomacy. Truth-and-reconciliation commissions engage the community—including victims of crime and nongovernmental organizations—in negotiating repair of harm with offenders who accept responsibility.

Track-two diplomacy raises valid questions about the impact of independent, uncoordinated efforts—especially when they appear at odds with formal government diplomacy. Yet, given the seemingly impossible challenges that traditional diplomacy faces, one can imagine an ideal world where unofficial diplomacy supports official efforts, perhaps even creating the conditions necessary for their success.

The rise of multitrack collaboration and the professions shaping modern peacebuilding.

When informal initiatives relieve some of the pressure on formal systems and correct their failings, institutional systems are freed to strengthen capacity and realize their ideals. The multistakeholder process behind Europe’s Convention on Access to Information, Public Participation in Decision-Making, and Justice in Environmental Matters stands as a model for democratized multilateral policymaking—an example of how global institutions can emulate the spirit of participation and transparency.

Which professions are most motivated to make the commitment to track-two diplomacy and play this essential leadership role? Unfortunately, many nongovernmental organizations appear more renowned for their competition over funding than for proactive community partnering and effective peacebuilding. Fortunately, there are others—such as the International Red Cross—whose reputation and role remain strong.

Faith, Labor, and Moral Courage

History shows that despite religion’s role in fueling war, prejudice, and hatred, religious leaders of many different faiths often organize against the worst of exploitation and oppression. At times, labor leaders join them, demonstrating the moral courage and influence needed to advance their communities’ deepest needs.

Occasionally, grieving parents—such as those who founded Mothers Against Drunk Driving or the Women Waging Peace Network after losing loved ones to war—emerge from sorrow to lead movements, speak truth to power, and transform the conditions of their tragedy. One might imagine that more social workers, politicians, and law-enforcement officers could be moved to serve their communities in similar ways—and many do, quietly, through daily unrecognized service.

In some war-torn societies, doctors and nurses become surprisingly trusted, impartial mediators—though often unequipped or untrained to respond effectively to conflict or lead community change. While the public celebrates heroes, leaders in peacebuilding and conflict resolution never act alone. Like Desmond Tutu during South Africa’s apartheid, they build critical alliances and rally collective support.

Unsung Heroes and Modern Parallels

How courage, conscience, and communication shape peace across generations.

Gandhi, once an unsuccessful Oxford-educated lawyer who failed to establish a practice in India, was nevertheless well prepared for clever rebuttals when later interrogated in British courts. More importantly, he became a sharp strategist—aligning with Western media and inspiring the Indian masses toward unity and justice.

It is likely that there are many unsung heroes of social justice and peacebuilding that most will never know. Introducing Skillful Negotiation

How strategy, persuasion, and empathy turn conflict into collaboration.

Negotiation embraces a wide range of strategies and tactics designed to persuade, influence agreement, create value, solve problems, and resolve disputes. Bargaining can occur between two parties or among many—whether domestic or international. In truth, we all negotiate daily and often with ourselves: what to prioritize, how to treat one another, and how to allocate time and attention.

Distributive and Integrative Negotiation

From fixed-pie thinking to creative, interest-based collaboration.

Between the two offers, negotiators engage in a predictable dance of proportional and responsive concessions until they reach agreement through compromise. Most negotiations, however, do not merely involve a “fixed pie” to be divided. They encompass complex layers of interests and needs that must be explored, identified, and satisfied—rather than simply split.

Integrative bargaining, in contrast to distributive bargaining, assumes interdependence. It is designed to handle complexity and expects that different stakeholders will define desired outcomes in their own subjective ways. It does not equate such differences in perspective with adversarial positioning. Instead, diverse perspectives are studied and reframed in search of options that maximize satisfaction without requiring a change in mandate.

Insight — Roger Fisher’s Contribution

Roger Fisher, founder of the Harvard Negotiation Project, introduced and popularized interest analysis as an alternative to positional or power-based bargaining. Rather than expecting political leaders to abandon their postures, he analyzed them—and then used the principals’ underlying interests to stimulate creative problem solving.

In contrast, framing a complex conflict with linear logic as a simple “either/or” win–lose paradigm polarizes the parties. Overly confident distributive moves risk escalating hostility, eroding trust, and inciting unproductive reactions—especially in conflicts involving groups.

A study of U.S. lawyers described the least effective negotiators with words such as arrogant, stubborn, unethical, and egotistic. Likewise, a global study of multicultural leadership in conflict processes identified the least effective as closed-minded, judgmental, insensitive, negative, and indifferent. Even when a party succeeds in forcing its desired outcome, the agreement is unlikely to last—and the risk of public scrutiny or backlash is high.

International Application

Distributive bargaining in the international arena makes sense only with truly fixed-resource situations, and even then it must be exercised with skill to avoid costly fallout. Case studies from the United Nations and other organizations show that integrative approaches produce superior outcomes. Agreements reached by consensus are more sustainable and easier to implement than those imposed by force.

Mediators—facilitators of negotiation—are increasingly recognized as adding essential sophistication when parties lack experience with integrative bargaining. In a global study of multicultural leadership, researchers found that careful listening alone can advance progress, particularly in cross-ethnic negotiation.

Proven Research and Theories in Negotiation

What the data reveal about trust, cooperation, and transforming aggression into collaboration.

The Afghan student’s perspective asserts that after a long history of distributive failures, resolution became possible only after finally attempting interest-based negotiation for the first time. Sadly, the situation has since deteriorated after twenty years of U.S. military intervention.

Proven Research and Theories

Much negotiation research and theory has been criticized for lacking practical usefulness. Short-term laboratory studies fail to simulate complex, real-world conflict. Howard Raiffa, a renowned game theorist, found during his international administrative work that negotiators often fail to act in rational or coherent ways, undermining the predictive power of purely rational models.

A few studies, however, are noteworthy exceptions. While many rational theories cannot predict diverse cultural or irrational behavior, they can still offer insights that make a critical difference in outcomes.

Robert Axelrod — Transforming Aggression to Cooperation

Axelrod’s mathematical research sought optimal strategies for turning aggressive tactics into collaborative bargaining. His findings were striking: beginning negotiations cooperatively and giving generously builds goodwill. Otherwise, a defensive, hostile climate quickly forms. Once trust is broken, it is far harder to repair than to build from the start.

Yet, goodwill cannot be assumed. Negotiators must remain vigilant, responding strategically to each countermove—as in a game of chess. If an opponent acts aggressively, a proportional response can signal resilience and deter exploitation. Then, extending an olive branch invites renewed collaboration. When the invitation is accepted, hostility is transformed into cooperation—at least for the moment.

If the opponent stubbornly persists in aggression, however, the negotiator must resist firmly rather than yield.

Neil Rackham — What the Best Negotiators Do

Neil Rackham’s research on labor–management negotiations identified specific behaviors shared by the most effective negotiators—those whose agreements proved durable and were judged fair by all parties.

  • They were excellent gatherers of information, asking many more questions than their less effective counterparts.
  • They listened actively and attentively to what was said and left unsaid.
  • They zealously sought common ground and creative solutions that benefited all sides.

In contrast, less effective negotiators often resorted to unstrategic attack, wasting energy rehearsing arguments in their minds. As a result, they failed to observe or hear valuable information that could have guided them to success.
